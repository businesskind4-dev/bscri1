## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Purpose

The Botswana Supply Chain Readiness Index (BSCRI) is a comprehensive assessment framework designed to measure an organization's capability to compete effectively in regional and international supply chains.

BSCRI was developed specifically for the Botswana context, with particular attention to the opportunities and challenges presented by:

- The African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA)
- Botswana's landlocked geography
- The need for economic diversification
- The imperative to develop local SME capacity

### 1.2 What BSCRI Measures

BSCRI measures **supply chain competitiveness**, not just operational capability.

**Operational capability** answers: "Can you run your supply chain effectively?"

**Supply chain competitiveness** answers: "Can you compete regionally, withstand disruptions, and grow sustainably?"

BSCRI assesses the full spectrum of capabilities required for an organization to participate successfully in modern supply chains, from local procurement to cross-border trade.

## 2. Definition of Readiness

### 2.1 The Perfect Score

> **"An organization scoring 100 on BSCRI demonstrates the capabilities, governance, resilience, and market readiness required to compete effectively in regional and international supply chains."**

This definition anchors the entire framework. Every dimension, question, score, maturity level, and recommendation ultimately supports this definition.

### 2.2 What Readiness Means in Practice

A "ready" organization is one that can:

- Source goods and services strategically and ethically
- Move products efficiently across borders and within Botswana
- Comply with all regulatory requirements
- Leverage technology to improve supply chain performance
- Build and maintain strong supplier relationships
- Withstand disruptions and adapt to change
- Identify and pursue regional market opportunities

## 3. The 7 Dimensions of Supply Chain Readiness

BSCRI assesses organizations across **7 dimensions** of supply chain readiness. These dimensions represent the critical capabilities required for competitiveness in the Botswana and regional context.

### 3.1 Dimension 1: Procurement Capability

**Definition:** The ability to source goods and services strategically, manage supplier relationships, negotiate effectively, and achieve value for money while maintaining ethical standards.

**Why It Matters:**
Procurement represents 60–80% of costs in many organizations. Effective procurement drives profitability, ensures quality, and reduces risk. In Botswana, where many organizations rely on imported goods, procurement capability is essential for cost management and supply continuity.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Strategic sourcing and category management
- Contract negotiation and management
- Supplier evaluation and performance monitoring
- Ethical procurement and anti-corruption practices
- Procurement technology and systems

---

### 3.2 Dimension 2: Logistics & Operations

**Definition:** The ability to efficiently manage the flow of goods, from supplier to customer, including transportation, warehousing, inventory management, and last-mile delivery.

**Why It Matters:**
Botswana is landlocked. Efficient logistics is not a luxury—it is a survival requirement. Organizations that cannot manage logistics effectively face higher costs, longer lead times, and reduced competitiveness. Logistics capability determines whether a business can serve local markets and reach regional customers.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Transportation management (road, rail, air)
- Warehouse and distribution center operations
- Inventory management and optimization
- Last-mile delivery capability
- Logistics technology and tracking systems

---

### 3.3 Dimension 3: Compliance & Governance

**Definition:** The ability to meet all regulatory requirements, adhere to procurement policies, and maintain strong governance structures that ensure transparency and accountability.

**Why It Matters:**
Botswana has a strong regulatory environment. Organizations that fail to comply face penalties, reputational damage, and exclusion from public and private sector opportunities. Compliance is also a prerequisite for participation in regional trade agreements like AfCFTA.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Understanding of relevant regulations (BURS, BQA, etc.)
- Procurement policy adherence
- Contract and legal compliance
- Anti-corruption measures
- Governance and oversight structures

---

### 3.4 Dimension 4: Digital Capability

**Definition:** The ability to leverage technology and digital tools to improve supply chain visibility, efficiency, and decision-making.

**Why It Matters:**
The world is digitizing. Organizations that cannot adopt and use digital tools will be left behind. In Botswana, digital transformation is a national priority. Supply chains are increasingly dependent on data, automation, and real-time visibility.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Supply chain software and systems
- Data collection and analysis
- Digital communication with suppliers and customers
- E-procurement capability
- Cybersecurity awareness

---

### 3.5 Dimension 5: Supplier & Partnership Ecosystem

**Definition:** The ability to build, manage, and sustain strong relationships with suppliers and partners, including local content development and supplier diversity.

**Why It Matters:**
No organization operates in isolation. Strong supplier relationships enable better pricing, quality, and innovation. In Botswana, supplier development and local content are policy priorities. Organizations that invest in their suppliers contribute to national economic development.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Supplier relationship management
- Local supplier development
- Supplier diversity and inclusion
- Collaborative planning
- Partnership and alliance management

---

### 3.6 Dimension 6: Resilience & Risk

**Definition:** The ability to anticipate, withstand, and recover from supply chain disruptions, including border delays, currency fluctuations, single-source dependency, and regional instability.

**Why It Matters:**
Botswana's supply chains are vulnerable. Border delays can halt operations. Currency fluctuations can erode margins. Single-source dependency can create catastrophic risk. Organizations that are not resilient cannot survive long-term. Resilience is not optional—it is essential.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Risk identification and assessment
- Business continuity planning
- Supply chain diversification
- Supplier failure contingency
- Currency and financial risk management

---

### 3.7 Dimension 7: Trade & Growth Readiness

**Definition:** The ability to identify, access, and compete in regional and international markets, including export readiness, cross-border logistics, and understanding of trade agreements.

**Why It Matters:**
AfCFTA is creating unprecedented opportunities. Organizations that can trade across borders will grow. Those that cannot will be left behind. Trade readiness is not just about exporting—it is about participating in regional value chains, sourcing regionally, and competing with regional players.

**Key Capabilities:**
- Export documentation and procedures
- Cross-border logistics and customs
- Knowledge of regional markets
- Understanding of trade agreements (AfCFTA, SADC)
- International standards and certification
- Trade finance access

## 4. Primary Audience

### 4.1 Version 1: SMEs and Growing Businesses

BSCRI Version 1 is designed primarily for **SMEs and growing businesses in Botswana**.

**Why SMEs?**
- They need guidance most
- They are underserved by existing resources
- They align with Botswana's economic diversification goals
- They are the future suppliers entering larger supply chains
- They are the engines of job creation

### 4.2 Future Audiences

After Version 1 validation, BSCRI will be adapted for:

- **Large enterprises** (with more complex supply chains)
- **Government entities** (with procurement and policy focus)
- **Non-profit organizations** (with humanitarian supply chains)
- **Sector-specific versions** (Mining, Agriculture, Retail, etc.)

## 5. How the Framework Was Developed

BSCRI was developed through:

1. **Review of international supply chain frameworks** (GSCF, SCOR, and other models)
2. **Analysis of the Botswana context** (geography, economy, policy environment)
3. **Consultation with industry practitioners** and academics
4. **Alignment with national development goals** (NDP 11, Economic Diversification Drive)
5. **Consideration of AfCFTA requirements** and regional trade dynamics
