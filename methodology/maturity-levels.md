## 1. Overview

BSCRI maturity levels translate scores into meaningful categories that organizations can understand and act upon.

### 1.1 Purpose

Maturity levels serve three purposes:

| Purpose | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Clarity** | Turn abstract scores into concrete descriptions |
| **Motivation** | Show organizations where they are and where they can go |
| **Action** | Provide clear direction for improvement |

### 1.2 The Five Levels

| Level | Score Range | Color Code | Label |
|-------|-------------|------------|-------|
| 1 | 0–40 | Red | Foundational |
| 2 | 41–55 | Orange | Emerging |
| 3 | 56–70 | Yellow | Developing |
| 4 | 71–85 | Blue | Established |
| 5 | 86–100 | Green | Advanced |

## 2. Level 1: Foundational

### 2.1 Summary

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Score Range** | 0–40 |
| **Label** | Foundational |
| **Color** | Red |
| **Status** | Critical gaps exist |

### 2.2 What This Level Means

Organizations at this level have significant gaps in their supply chain capabilities. They are vulnerable to disruptions, likely missing opportunities, and at risk of being left behind by competitors.

### 2.3 Characteristics

**At this level, organizations typically:**
- Have no formal procurement processes
- Operate with limited or no logistics systems
- Are unaware of regulatory requirements
- Use minimal or no digital tools
- Rely on a few or single suppliers
- Have no risk management or business continuity plans
- Have no experience with cross-border trade

### 2.4 What This Looks Like

**Example - A Small Retail Business:**
> A retail shop in Maun that buys stock informally from wholesalers in South Africa. They have no formal supplier agreements, track inventory manually, and have never exported. A border delay or currency fluctuation could put them out of business.

### 2.5 What to Do Next

Organizations at this level should:
1. Document basic procurement processes
2. Establish relationships with multiple suppliers
3. Learn about regulatory requirements
4. Implement basic inventory tracking
5. Create a simple business continuity plan

### 2.6 Time to Progress

**Typical time to reach Emerging:** 6–12 months with focused effort.

## 3. Level 2: Emerging

### 3.1 Summary

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Score Range** | 41–55 |
| **Label** | Emerging |
| **Color** | Orange |
| **Status** | Gaps exist, action needed |

### 3.2 What This Level Means

Organizations at this level have begun building supply chain capabilities but still have significant gaps. They are moving in the right direction but are not yet competitive regionally.

### 3.3 Characteristics

**At this level, organizations typically:**
- Have informal procurement processes
- Have basic logistics arrangements
- Are somewhat familiar with regulations
- Use basic digital tools (spreadsheets)
- Have a few supplier relationships
- Have some risk awareness but no formal plans
- Have limited or no cross-border experience

### 3.4 What This Looks Like

**Example - A Growing SME:**
> A manufacturing business in Gaborone that has formal supplier contracts for key materials, uses spreadsheets to track inventory, and is aware of BURS requirements. They have one international supplier and have considered exporting but haven't taken steps.

### 3.5 What to Do Next

Organizations at this level should:
1. Formalize procurement processes
2. Implement basic inventory management
3. Ensure full regulatory compliance
4. Adopt supply chain software
5. Diversify supplier base
6. Develop a risk management plan
7. Explore cross-border opportunities

### 3.6 Time to Progress

**Typical time to reach Developing:** 6–12 months with focused effort.

## 4. Level 3: Developing

### 4.1 Summary

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Score Range** | 56–70 |
| **Label** | Developing |
| **Color** | Yellow |
| **Status** | On track but room for improvement |

### 4.2 What This Level Means

Organizations at this level have built solid supply chain foundations. They are functioning effectively but are not yet fully optimized or competitive regionally. They have strong potential to advance.

### 4.3 Characteristics

**At this level, organizations typically:**
- Have formal procurement processes
- Have organized warehouse and logistics operations
- Comply with most regulations
- Use dedicated supply chain software
- Have established supplier relationships
- Have a business continuity plan
- Have some cross-border experience

### 4.4 What This Looks Like

**Example - A Mid-Sized Company:**
> A distribution company in Francistown that uses an ERP system for inventory, has formal supplier contracts, complies with all regulations, and has a risk management plan. They have exported to South Africa and Namibia.

### 4.5 What to Do Next

Organizations at this level should:
1. Optimize procurement processes
2. Implement inventory forecasting
3. Achieve full regulatory compliance
4. Integrate supply chain systems
5. Strengthen supplier partnerships
6. Test and update business continuity plans
7. Expand regional presence

### 4.6 Time to Progress

**Typical time to reach Established:** 12–18 months with focused effort.

## 5. Level 4: Established

### 5.1 Summary

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Score Range** | 71–85 |
| **Label** | Established |
| **Color** | Blue |
| **Status** | Strong capability |

### 5.2 What This Level Means

Organizations at this level have strong supply chain capabilities. They are competitive locally and increasingly competitive regionally. They are well-positioned to take advantage of AfCFTA opportunities.

### 5.3 Characteristics

**At this level, organizations typically:**
- Have mature procurement processes
- Have optimized logistics operations
- Exceed regulatory requirements
- Use integrated digital systems
- Have strategic supplier partnerships
- Have tested business continuity plans
- Have established regional presence

### 5.4 What This Looks Like

**Example - A Regional Business:**
> A manufacturing company that sources regionally, operates across Southern Africa, has integrated supply chain systems, maintains ISO certifications, collaborates closely with suppliers, and actively participates in regional trade.

### 5.5 What to Do Next

Organizations at this level should:
1. Pursue continuous improvement
2. Expand regional and international markets
3. Adopt advanced technologies (AI, IoT)
4. Develop supplier innovation programs
5. Build resilience through diversification
6. Achieve international certifications
7. Become a reference organization

### 5.6 Time to Progress

**Typical time to reach Advanced:** 18–24 months with strategic focus.

## 6. Level 5: Advanced

### 6.1 Summary

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Score Range** | 86–100 |
| **Label** | Advanced |
| **Color** | Green |
| **Status** | World-class capability |

### 6.2 What This Level Means

Organizations at this level have world-class supply chain capabilities. They are fully competitive regionally and internationally. They demonstrate excellence across all dimensions.

### 6.3 Characteristics

**At this level, organizations typically:**
- Have world-class procurement practices
- Have fully optimized logistics operations
- Set compliance standards for their industry
- Leverage advanced digital technologies
- Have strategic global partnerships
- Have resilient supply chains
- Are leaders in regional and international trade

### 6.4 What This Looks Like

**Example - A Global Leader:**
> A Botswana-headquartered company that operates across Africa and internationally, uses AI-driven supply chain systems, holds multiple international certifications, actively shapes industry standards, and is a reference for supply chain excellence.

### 6.5 What to Do Next

Organizations at this level should:
1. Maintain and enhance excellence
2. Share knowledge with the ecosystem
3. Mentor other organizations
4. Contribute to policy development
5. Drive innovation in supply chains

### 6.6 Maintaining This Level

**Recommended actions:**
- Annual reassessment
- Continuous improvement culture
- Benchmarking against global best practices
- Industry leadership and advocacy
